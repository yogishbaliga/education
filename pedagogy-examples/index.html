Five Ways to Teach the Same Lesson

The same topic — graphing coordinates — taught five different ways. Each approach has a different theory of how learning sticks.

6th Grade · Graphing Coordinates
1
Explore First
Hook → Discover → Name

Students play with the coordinate grid before the concept is named. The label "ordered pair" arrives as an explanation of what they already noticed, not as a definition to memorize.

Based on the generation effect — brains that struggle first remember the explanation better.

2
Direct Instruction
Define → Model → Practice

Concept and vocabulary come first, explicitly. The teacher (this page) models three worked examples, then hands off to guided practice, then independent practice.

Based on Rosenshine's Principles — high-effect-size approach especially for students with limited prior knowledge.

3
Concrete → Pictorial → Abstract
CPA · Singapore Math

Three distinct stages: first a city-map metaphor students can touch and relate to; then a visual coordinate grid; then pure (x, y) notation. Students cannot skip stages.

Based on Bruner's CPA model — widely used in Singapore's top-ranked math curriculum.

Concrete map → Grid diagram → (x, y) symbols Practice

4
Three-Act Structure
Dan Meyer's Model

Act 1 poses an unresolvable mystery — a location on an unlabeled grid. Act 2 lets students request information piece by piece. Act 3 resolves the mystery and introduces the formal math.

Designed to make students want the concept before they receive it.

5
Worked Example Fading
Cognitive Load Theory

Four problems, each with less scaffolding than the last. Problem 1 is fully solved for you. Problem 4 is completely independent. The transition is gradual so the "blank page" never appears suddenly.

Based on Sweller's Cognitive Load Theory — reduces frustration at the guided-to-independent handoff.
